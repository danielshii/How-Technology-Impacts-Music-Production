Annotated Bibliography

 

D'Errico, M. (2022). Push: Software design and the Cultural Politics of Music Production. Oxford University Press.

Mike DErrico, Assistant Professor of Music and Computer Science at Albright College, discusses how advancements in technology have facilitated the transition from traditional methods of music production to more modern ways of creating music. Arguably the most significant aspect of this transition that is a huge focus of my own project is the rise of MIDI controllers and DAWs (Digital Audio Workspaces). A DAW compacts the entire production process into a single piece of software, making music production more portable and accessible than ever. Likewise, MIDI Controllers, when operating in tandem with a DAW, bring back the humanity of a piece of music that may otherwise have been lost to the robotic nature of a lone DAW. While DErrico discusses DAWs in a very broad and positive light, Duignan delves deeper into the mechanisms of a DAW and opposes DErrico by listing certain creative and technological limitations of a DAW with regards to music production.

 

Duignan, M., Noble, J., & Biddle, R. (2010). Abstraction and Activity in Computer-Mediated Music Production. Computer Music Journal, 34(4), 2233. https://doi.org/10.1162/COMJ_a_00023

University of Wellington Professor Matthew Duignan discusses various features of a traditional DAW (Digital Audio Workspace)a software tool that facilitates the music production processas well as its current limitations. Most of the journals research comes as a result of interviewing producers and artists who are proficient in operating DAWs; however, the anonymity of the participants in this journal might serve as a source of bias because the reader cant accurately gauge how proficient the participants might be since theres no established credibility for them. One interesting limitation that the journal touches on is the lack of a DAWs ability to humanize a song. Since most DAWs operate as a grid-like structure for producers to drag and drop notes and such, many musicians might find that their productions might sound too robotic or that the rhythm of their song is too perfect. In order to work around this issue, producers have to drag notes slightly in front or behind the lines of a grid to throw off the robotic timing and give the illusion that a human being is performing the song. In fact, one participant in the journal stated that this process is often the most time-consuming. What the journal doesnt consider, however, is the existence of particular shortcuts that certain DAWs possess that allow producers to humanize their production. For example, the DAW FL Studio has a feature that allows a producer to adjust the swing of a song. This can give a traditional, quantized drum pattern more of a jazzy feel and thus gives it an element of humanity. However, by listing certain limitations of DAWs, Duignan is able to counter DErrico and highlight the fact that musical technology has room to improve.

 

 

Strachan, R. (2017). Sonic Technologies: Popular Music, Digital Culture and the creative process. Bloomsbury Academic.

 

Robert Strachan, a lecturer at the University of Liverpools School of Music, touches on the changes and advancements that technology has brought to the music production process. Like DErrico and Duignan, Strachan also elaborates on the modern technologies of music production such as DAWs. However, it brings some new discussions to the table regarding DAWs, such as VSTs, third-party plugins capable of enhancing the existing power of a DAW. Strachan also attributes beginner-friendliness as another reason for DAWs popularity. As stated, the intuitiveness of a DAW is why learning music production is easier than ever. Whereas DErrico and Duignan discuss DAWs in a technical sense, Strachan elaborates more on the cultural impact of DAWs on the music scene and how their clever designs have appealed to producers everywhere, thereby making music production accessible to a larger audience.

 

 

Bell, Adam Patrick. Trial-by-Fire: A Case Study of the Musician-Engineer Hybrid Role in the Home Studio. Journal of music, technology and education 7.3 (2014): 295312. Web.

 

Adam Bell, a professor at Montclair State University, walks us through the entire producing and songwriting process for a typical music artist who is also the engineer. Bell prefaces his article by outlining the reasons behind the home studios rise in popularity. Before, engineers and artists were largely considered separate roles, each managed by a different personthe engineer creates the music, the artist sings over it. However, thanks to modern technology that makes music production far more accessible and simpler, were starting to see a shift towards what Bell likes to call the Musician-Engineer. Nowadays, a computer is all a producer needs to make a song, so why invest in an expensive studio when you can easily create a hit from your own bedroom? This rhetorical question is the primary spur for the home studio. Bell then spends the rest of his time walking the reader through the entire recording and songwriting process from the perspective of an independent artist, who oversees the process by themselves. The growing popularity of the home studio can be largely attributed to the accessibility and portability of modern technology, something that Strachan elaborates on more. On the other hand, DErrico argues that although a shift towards the home studio was made possible with new musical technology, the shift isnt culturally significant, as record labels still hold lots of power when it comes to making an artist go big. In other words, its significantly harder for an independent artist to put themselves out there as opposed to having a record label behind them to sell/promote their product.
